## Script

**[0:00–0:12 — Hook]**

Should you make a will or a living trust in Texas? If someone gives you the same answer for every family, they are skipping the facts that actually matter. Here are the questions that can help you find a sensible starting point.

**[0:12–0:26 — Scope]**

This is general education, not legal advice. A Texas attorney can apply the law to your property, family, and goals. We will keep this comparison practical and avoid pretending one document always wins.

**[0:26–0:51 — What a will does]**

A will can state who should receive probate property, name an executor, and nominate guardians for minor children. It takes effect at death and generally works through probate. But it does not control every asset. Beneficiary designations, survivorship arrangements, and property held in trust may pass another way.

**[0:51–1:18 — What a living trust does]**

A revocable living trust is a structure created during life. The person making it can generally change it while capable. A successor trustee can manage property held in the trust under its terms. After death, properly funded trust property may be administered outside the probate process. The important words are “property held in the trust.”

**[1:18–1:48 — Question one and two]**

Start with real estate. Do you own a home or other property? Do you own real estate outside Texas? Multiple states can create additional administration questions. A trust may deserve a closer conversation, but title, debt, tax, and homestead issues still need individual review.

**[1:48–2:14 — Complexity and privacy]**

Next, consider family and planning complexity. A blended family, a business, a beneficiary with disabilities, or detailed distribution goals can make careful drafting more important. Also ask how much probate avoidance and privacy matter to you. Those goals can support discussing a trust, but they do not eliminate the need for coordinated documents.

**[2:14–2:43 — Funding tradeoff]**

Then ask the question many comparisons leave out: are you prepared to fund and maintain the trust? Signing a trust does not automatically move every asset into it. Real estate, accounts, business interests, and beneficiary designations may need separate coordination. An unfunded trust cannot deliver the result people expect from it.

**[2:43–3:05 — The shared foundation]**

Whichever structure you discuss first, the broader plan may still need financial and medical powers of attorney, healthcare instructions, beneficiary reviews, and a practical asset inventory. A trust is not a substitute for every incapacity document, and a will is not the only part of a will-based plan.
